The Case for
Strength Training

Why resistance training is the single most evidence-backed investment you can make in your long-term health — and why almost everyone who needs it most isn't doing it.

What this guide is about

This is not a fitness manifesto. It's not a sales pitch for the gym. And it's not about getting "ripped" or "shredded" or whatever word the fitness industry is using this year.

This is a summary of what the research actually says about what happens to your body when you do — and don't — engage in regular resistance training. Strength training. Lifting things. Working your muscles against force.

The findings are unambiguous: muscle is not a vanity organ. It is a survival organ. It protects your bones, your brain, your heart, your metabolism, your immune system, and your ability to live independently. Losing it — which you are doing, right now, whether you feel it or not — is one of the most reliable predictors of disease, disability, and death.

The good news: you can reverse the decline. And it takes far less time and effort than you think.

Every claim in this guide is backed by peer-reviewed research. Citations are included at the end. None of this is controversial in the scientific community — the evidence is settled. What's missing is public awareness.

That's what this guide is for.

The problem: you're losing muscle right now

After age 30, the average adult loses 3–8% of their muscle mass per decade. This process — called sarcopenia — accelerates after 60. By the time you're in your 70s, you may have lost 25–30% of the muscle you had at your peak.

You can't feel it happening. There's no warning. Muscles don't send an alert saying "we're shrinking." You just gradually get weaker, slower, and more fragile — and you attribute it to "getting older" as if it's inevitable.

It's not inevitable. It's preventable.

3–8%

Muscle mass lost per decade after age 30

Inactive adults also lose resting metabolic rate (↓7%) and gain fat (↑1.8 kg) over the same period. Ten weeks of resistance training reverses all three.

Westcott, Current Sports Medicine Reports, 2012

Why does muscle loss matter so much? Because muscle isn't just for moving. It's an active organ that:

  • Burns calories — muscle is metabolically active tissue. Less muscle means a slower metabolism, which means more fat accumulation.
  • Stores glucose — skeletal muscle accounts for 80% of insulin-stimulated glucose uptake. Less muscle means worse blood sugar control.
  • Protects your bones — muscles pull on bones, stimulating bone formation. When muscles weaken, bones weaken.
  • Fights inflammation — contracting muscle releases anti-inflammatory molecules called myokines. Less muscle means more chronic inflammation.
  • Supports your brain — muscle-derived molecules (including BDNF) support neurogenesis and cognitive function.
  • Keeps you upright — leg and core strength are your primary defense against falls, which are the leading cause of injury death in older adults.

Losing muscle isn't one problem. It's the root of many problems. And resistance training is the only intervention that directly addresses it.

What the research shows

1. You will live longer

This is the headline finding, and it's striking: muscular strength is one of the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality. Not body weight. Not BMI. Not how much cardio you do. How strong you are.

78%

Lower all-cause mortality in the strongest men

In a landmark study of 8,762 men followed for 19 years, those in the highest third of muscular strength had 60–78% lower risk of death from all causes — even after adjusting for age, physical activity, smoking, alcohol, BMI, and comorbidities.

Ruiz et al., BMJ, 2008

This wasn't a fluke. A 2018 meta-analysis of approximately 2 million men and women confirmed the association: higher muscular strength was consistently linked to 10–17% lower mortality risk, in both sexes, across all age groups.

~2 million

People in the largest meta-analysis on strength and mortality

Higher muscular strength was associated with 10–17% lower risk of all-cause mortality in both men and women, independent of age, BMI, and lifestyle factors.

García-Hermoso et al., Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation, 2018

Even your grip strength alone — a simple measurement anyone can do — predicts your risk of dying from any cause, from cancer, and from cardiovascular disease. There are established thresholds: below certain grip strength values, mortality risk increases sharply.

The dose-response relationship between handgrip strength and mortality is clear: stronger is better, and there appears to be no upper limit to the benefit.
— López-Bueno et al., Ageing Research Reviews, 2022

2. You will feel better

Depression is one of the most common and debilitating conditions in the world. Resistance training helps — significantly.

33 RCTs

Demonstrating resistance training reduces depression

A JAMA Psychiatry meta-analysis of 33 randomized controlled trials found that resistance training significantly reduced depressive symptoms — regardless of health status, training volume, or whether participants had a formal depression diagnosis. Effect sizes were comparable to psychotherapy and pharmacotherapy for mild-to-moderate depression.

Gordon et al., JAMA Psychiatry, 2018

This is not "exercise makes you feel good" hand-waving. This is a rigorous meta-analysis published in one of psychiatry's top journals, showing that a specific type of exercise — resistance training — produces clinically meaningful reductions in depression.

The effect is not limited to diagnosed depression. It works for subclinical symptoms, for older adults, for young people, and across all severity levels. You don't need to be "depressed" to benefit. You just need to be human.

Resistance training also reduces anxiety, improves sleep quality, and enhances overall quality of life — effects that have been confirmed across dozens of randomized trials.

3. Your brain will work better

Cognitive decline is one of the fears that haunts aging. Resistance training directly addresses it.

12+ months

Cognitive benefits persist after training stops

A 12-month RCT in women aged 65–75 found that once- and twice-weekly resistance training improved executive functions (planning, decision-making, task-switching). The improvements persisted 12 months after the training program ended.

Liu-Ambrose et al., Archives of Internal Medicine, 2010

How does this work? One key mechanism is BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) — a protein that supports the growth and survival of neurons. Resistance training increases BDNF levels. It also improves cerebral blood flow, reduces neuroinflammation, and promotes neuroplasticity.

Perhaps most remarkably, leg strength alone has been shown to predict cognitive function years later. The muscles in your legs appear to send signals that keep your brain healthy. When your legs get weak, your brain follows.

4. Your metabolism will work

Type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, obesity — these are all, at their core, disorders of glucose metabolism. And skeletal muscle is where the majority of glucose disposal happens.

80%

Of insulin-stimulated glucose uptake occurs in skeletal muscle

Skeletal muscle is the primary tissue responsible for clearing glucose from the bloodstream after a meal. More muscle means greater glucose storage capacity and better insulin sensitivity.

Whytock & Goodpaster, Circulation Research, 2025

When you lose muscle, you lose your body's primary glucose sink. Blood sugar creeps up. Insulin resistance develops. The path to type 2 diabetes shortens.

Resistance training reverses this. It increases the number of glucose transporters (GLUT4) in your muscle cells, improves glycogen storage capacity, and enhances mitochondrial function. For people with type 2 diabetes, resistance training is as effective as — and in some studies superior to — aerobic exercise for improving blood sugar control.

The critical finding for anyone who has ever dieted: A New England Journal of Medicine study showed that in obese older adults, those who lost weight through diet alone or diet + aerobic exercise lost significant muscle and bone mass. Those who added resistance training lost fat while maintaining muscle and bone. If you lose weight without lifting, you become a smaller, weaker version of yourself. Resistance training ensures you become a stronger one.

5. Your bones won't break

Osteoporosis — the progressive weakening of bones — affects over 200 million people worldwide. It's the leading cause of fractures in older adults, and fractures are a leading cause of disability and death.

Resistance training is the only intervention that simultaneously strengthens muscle and bone. When muscles contract and pull on bone during resistance exercise, they stimulate osteoblast activity — the cells that build new bone.

0 injuries

In the LIFTMOR trial of high-intensity RT for osteoporosis

The LIFTMOR trial demonstrated that high-intensity resistance and impact training is safe even for postmenopausal women with osteoporosis — and actually increased bone mineral density, improved physical function, and reduced spinal kyphosis.

Watson et al., Journal of Bone and Mineral Research, 2018

This is important because many people with osteoporosis are told to "be careful" or avoid lifting heavy things. The LIFTMOR trial — and the UK consensus statement on exercise for osteoporosis — directly contradict this outdated advice. The fracture risk from inactivity far exceeds any risk from properly prescribed resistance exercise.

6. Your immune system will improve

In the last two decades, science has recognized that skeletal muscle is an endocrine organ — it secretes hundreds of signaling molecules called myokines that have effects throughout the body.

When you contract your muscles during resistance exercise, they release interleukin-6 (IL-6) — but unlike the IL-6 produced by infection or chronic disease (which is pro-inflammatory), muscle-derived IL-6 is anti-inflammatory. It stimulates the production of anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1ra, IL-10) and inhibits tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α).

This means resistance training directly combats chronic low-grade inflammation — the condition that underlies cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cancer, Alzheimer's, and most age-related diseases.

More muscle = more myokines = more anti-inflammatory protection. It's that simple.

7. It helps you survive cancer

International guidelines now recommend exercise — including resistance training — for all cancer survivors. The evidence supports:

  • Reduced cancer-related fatigue (a debilitating side effect of treatment)
  • Preservation of lean mass during chemotherapy
  • Improved quality of life and physical function
  • Reduced inflammatory markers associated with recurrence
  • Safe for breast cancer survivors — does not worsen lymphedema

The anti-inflammatory effects of muscle-derived myokines may also play a role in reducing cancer recurrence, though more research is needed on this specific question.

8. You'll sleep better

A systematic review and network meta-analysis of exercise and sleep quality in older adults found that resistance training was among the most effective modalities for improving sleep — comparable to or better than aerobic exercise.

An RCT in patients with chronic insomnia found that resistance training significantly improved sleep quality, sleep efficiency, and time to fall asleep — outperforming stretching and waitlist control.

The mechanisms likely involve adenosine accumulation, body temperature regulation, anxiety reduction, and increased slow-wave sleep. But the practical point is simple: lift things, sleep better.

9. It's incredibly safe

The most common objection to resistance training is fear of injury. The data says otherwise.

0.01

Injuries per 1,000 training hours

Resistance training has one of the lowest injury rates of any physical activity — lower than running (0.5–7.5), basketball (6–26), soccer, or recreational walking. Most injuries are minor strains from poor technique, not inherent risk.

Batterson et al., Mayo Clinic Proceedings, 2020; Serafim et al., J Orthopaedic Surgery and Research, 2023

Resistance training is safe across every population studied:

  • Youth: Does not stunt growth, damage growth plates, or cause excessive hypertrophy. Injury rate lower than most youth sports.
  • Elderly (75+): Significant gains in muscle and strength with rare/minor adverse events.
  • Women: Equivalent relative adaptations to men. Same programs, same safety profile.
  • Pregnancy: Recommended by Canadian guidelines — 3x/week moderate resistance training is safe.
  • Osteoporosis: The LIFTMOR trial showed high-intensity RT is safe with zero adverse events.
  • Cardiac patients: Safe and beneficial in cardiovascular rehabilitation.
  • Neurological conditions: Effective and safe for Parkinson's disease, stroke, and multiple sclerosis.

10. You don't need much time

This may be the most important finding for anyone who thinks they're too busy: the minimum effective dose is remarkably low.

1–2×/week

Minimum for significant health benefits

A network meta-analysis of 151 randomized trials found that even 1–2 sessions per week of resistance training produces significant improvements in lean body mass, strength, and functional capacity. The optimal dose is 2–3 sessions per week, 2–6 sets per muscle group.

Radaelli et al., Sports Medicine, 2025

You don't need to live in the gym. You don't need to train for hours. Two or three 30–45 minute sessions per week is enough to produce meaningful changes in muscle mass, strength, bone density, metabolic health, cognitive function, and mental health.

The best program is the one you'll actually do. And anything is infinitely better than nothing.

The bottom line

Here's what the research says, in plain language:

  • You are losing muscle right now. It's happening silently and it accelerates with age.
  • Muscle loss is a root cause of metabolic disease, cognitive decline, bone fractures, depression, and premature death.
  • Resistance training is the only intervention that directly reverses muscle loss.
  • It also improves your bones, brain, mood, sleep, immune function, and metabolic health.
  • It is extraordinarily safe — safer than running, walking, or most recreational activities.
  • You only need 1–2 sessions per week to see meaningful benefits.
  • It works for everyone — every age, every sex, every fitness level, every health condition.

This isn't a fringe opinion. It's the consensus of the scientific community, supported by hundreds of randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses published in the world's leading medical journals.

The question is not whether you should do resistance training.

The question is why you're not doing it yet.
