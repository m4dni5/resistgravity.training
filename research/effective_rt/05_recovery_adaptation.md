# Recovery and Adaptation

## Recovery and Adaptation

**Key Finding:** Recovery capacity does decline with age, but most healthy 40+ adults recover adequately from 2-3 RT sessions per week with 48-72 hours between sessions for the same muscle group. Sleep quality, nutrition (especially protein intake), and total life stress are stronger determinants of recovery than age alone. Inadequate sleep can significantly impair strength adaptations.

### Papers

**1. The influence of resistance exercise training prescription variables on skeletal muscle mass, strength, and physical function in healthy adults: An umbrella review.**
- Jonathan Mcleod, Brad Currier, Caroline Lowisz et al. (2024)
- *Journal of sport and health science*
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jshs.2023.06.005
- Summary: Umbrella review synthesizing evidence on RT prescription variables and their impact on muscle mass, strength, and physical function across the lifespan. Found that RT produces significant benefits at all ages. Training 2-3x/week with adequate intensity produces robust outcomes in healthy adults including older populations, with recovery being manageable at these frequencies.

**2. Systematic review and meta-analysis of protein intake to support muscle mass and function in healthy adults.**
- Everson Nunes, Lauren Colenso-Semple, Sean McKellar et al. (2022)
- *Journal of cachexia, sarcopenia and muscle*
- https://doi.org/10.1002/jcsm.12922
- Summary: Meta-analysis of protein intake and lean body mass outcomes. Found that higher protein intakes (≥1.2-1.6 g/kg/day) support greater lean mass gains and strength when combined with RT. Protein intake is a key recovery variable — inadequate protein can limit adaptation even when training stimulus is sufficient.

**3. Inadequate sleep and muscle strength: Implications for resistance training.**
- Olivia Knowles, Eric Drinkwater, Charles Urwin et al. (2018)
- *Journal of science and medicine in sport*
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jsams.2018.01.012
- Summary: Review examining the relationship between inadequate sleep and muscle strength outcomes. Found that sleep deprivation impairs force production, reduces testosterone and growth hormone secretion, and increases cortisol — all of which compromise RT adaptations. For 40+ adults balancing work and training, sleep quality may be the most important recovery variable.

**4. Nutritional and Pharmacological Interventions to Expedite Recovery Following Muscle-Damaging Exercise in Older Adults: A Narrative Review of the Literature.**
- Tom Clifford (2019)
- *Journal of aging and physical activity*
- https://doi.org/10.1123/japa.2018-0351
- Summary: Review of recovery strategies in older adults (≥50 years). Found that recovery from EIMD is impaired with age, with older adults experiencing prolonged soreness and force decrements. Protein supplementation, anti-inflammatory foods, and adequate sleep are evidence-based recovery strategies. NSAIDs may impair muscle adaptation and should be used cautiously.

**5. Effects of Resistance Training Volume on Physical Function, Lean Body Mass and Lower-Body Muscle Hypertrophy and Strength in Older Adults: A Systematic Review and Network Meta-analysis of 151 Randomised Trials.**
- Régis Radaelli, Anderson Rech, Talita Molinari et al. (2025)
- *Sports medicine (Auckland, N.Z.)*
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02123-z
- Summary: Large network meta-analysis of 151 trials in older adults. Found that moderate volume RT (not maximum volume) produced optimal outcomes for physical function and lean mass. Higher volumes did not proportionally increase benefits and may compromise recovery in older populations. Training duration ≥12 weeks was associated with greater effects.

**6. Evolution of sarcopenia status and risk of incident cardiovascular disease.**
- Shuang Bing, Zhanchao Chen, Di Wu et al. (2025)
- *European journal of preventive cardiology*
- https://doi.org/10.1093/eurjpc/zwaf115
- Summary: Investigated how changes in sarcopenia status affect cardiovascular disease risk. Found that maintaining or improving muscle mass through RT reduced CVD risk, while loss of muscle mass increased it. Reinforces that consistent RT is important for long-term health maintenance in aging adults, and breaks in training can reverse health benefits.
