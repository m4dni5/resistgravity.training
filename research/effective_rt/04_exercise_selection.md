# Exercise Selection and Movement Patterns

## Exercise Selection

**Key Finding:** Multi-joint compound movements (squat, hinge, push, pull) produce similar or superior strength and hypertrophy outcomes compared to single-joint exercises, with greater time efficiency. Free weights and machines produce comparable hypertrophy, though free weights may offer additional benefits for functional capacity and stabilization. The best exercise selection prioritizes major movement patterns and accounts for individual limitations.

### Papers

**1. Multi-joint vs. Single-joint Resistance Exercises Induce a Similar Strength Increase in Trained Men: A Randomized Longitudinal Crossover Study.**
- Felipe Brigatto, Júlio de Camargo, Witter de Ungaro et al. (2020)
- *International journal of exercise science*
- https://doi.org/10.70252/LLHU2255
- Summary: Crossover study comparing multi-joint and single-joint exercises on maximal strength in trained men. Found that both exercise types produced similar strength increases when sets and intensity were equated. However, multi-joint exercises are more time-efficient as they train multiple muscle groups simultaneously.

**2. Training specificity performing single-joint vs. multi-joint resistance exercises among physically active females: A randomized controlled trial.**
- Nicolay Stien, Helene Pedersen, Aril Ravnøy et al. (2020)
- *PloS one*
- https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0233540
- Summary: Compared leg press (multi-joint) vs. leg extension + kickback (single-joint) on dynamic and isometric strength. Multi-joint training produced greater improvements in the multi-joint test, while single-joint training was superior for single-joint tests. Highlights the principle of specificity — training should include movement patterns relevant to daily function.

**3. Effect of free-weight vs. machine-based strength training on maximal strength, hypertrophy and jump performance - a systematic review and meta-analysis.**
- Markus Haugen, Fredrik Vårvik, Stian Larsen et al. (2023)
- *BMC sports science, medicine & rehabilitation*
- https://doi.org/10.1186/s13102-023-00713-4
- Summary: Meta-analysis comparing free-weight and machine-based training. Found no significant differences in maximal strength or hypertrophy between modalities. Free weights showed a slight advantage for jump performance. For health-focused adults, both modalities are effective — choice can be based on preference, availability, and individual limitations.

**4. The Effect of Eccentric vs. Traditional Resistance Exercise on Muscle Strength, Body Composition, and Functional Performance in Older Adults: A Systematic Review With Meta-Analysis.**
- Klemen Čretnik, Jernej Pleša, Žiga Kozinc et al. (2022)
- *Frontiers in sports and active living*
- https://doi.org/10.3389/fspor.2022.873718
- Summary: Compared eccentric-focused vs. traditional resistance exercise in older adults. Eccentric training produced comparable or superior strength gains with potentially lower cardiovascular demand. Eccentric training may be particularly useful for older adults with joint limitations, as higher loads can be used at lower perceived effort.

**5. Suspension training vs. traditional resistance training: effects on muscle mass, strength and functional performance in older adults.**
- Samuel Soligon, Deivid da Silva, João Bergamasco et al. (2020)
- *European journal of applied physiology*
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-020-04446-x
- Summary: Compared suspension training (TRX-style) with traditional RT in untrained older adults. Both modalities produced similar gains in muscle mass, strength, and functional performance when sets and effort were equated. Supports the use of bodyweight/suspension training as a viable alternative for health outcomes.

**6. Resistance training prescription for muscle strength and hypertrophy in healthy adults: a systematic review and Bayesian network meta-analysis.**
- Brad Currier, Jonathan Mcleod, Laura Banfield et al. (2023)
- *British journal of sports medicine*
- https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2023-106807
- Summary: Bayesian network meta-analysis of RT prescription variables. Found that combinations of moderate-to-high load with higher volumes produced the greatest strength and hypertrophy outcomes. Exercise selection was less important than the fundamental variables of load, volume, and proximity to failure. Supports flexibility in exercise choice when fundamentals are optimized.
