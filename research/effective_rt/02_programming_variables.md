# Programming Variables: Volume, Frequency, Intensity, Progression

## Programming Variables

**Key Finding:** 2-3 sessions per muscle group per week with 3-6 sets per muscle group at moderate-to-high intensity (60-80% 1RM) produces robust strength and health outcomes. Progressive overload matters for continued adaptation, but can be achieved through adding reps OR load. Frequency of 2x/week appears slightly superior to 1x/week when volume is equated.

### Papers

**1. The Resistance Training Dose Response: Meta-Regressions Exploring the Effects of Weekly Volume and Frequency on Muscle Hypertrophy and Strength Gains.**
- Joshua Pelland, Jacob Remmert, Zac Robinson et al. (2026)
- *Sports medicine (Auckland, N.Z.)*
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02344-w
- Summary: Recent meta-regression examining the dose-response relationships of weekly set volume and training frequency on muscle hypertrophy and strength. Found that higher weekly set volume enhances both outcomes, with the relationship appearing approximately linear up to a point of diminishing returns. Frequency effects were largely mediated through volume distribution.

**2. How many times per week should a muscle be trained to maximize muscle hypertrophy? A systematic review and meta-analysis.**
- Brad Schoenfeld, Jozo Grgic, James Krieger (2019)
- *Journal of sports sciences*
- https://doi.org/10.1080/02640414.2018.1555906
- Summary: Meta-analysis of training frequency effects on hypertrophy. When volume is equated, training a muscle 2x/week appears to produce slightly superior hypertrophy compared to 1x/week. However, the effect is modest, and total weekly volume is the primary driver of hypertrophic adaptations regardless of how it's distributed.

**3. A meta-analysis to determine the dose response for strength development.**
- Matthew Rhea, Brent Alvar, Lee Burkett et al. (2003)
- *Medicine and science in sports and exercise*
- https://doi.org/10.1249/01.MSS.0000053727.63505.D4
- Summary: Classic meta-analysis determining dose-response for strength training. Found that 60% of 1RM produced significant strength gains in untrained individuals, while 80% of 1RM was needed for trained individuals. Higher intensities and volumes produced greater strength gains, with the dose-response relationship being steeper for trained populations.

**4. Dose-Response Relationships of Resistance Training in Healthy Old Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.**
- Ron Borde, Tibor Hortobágyi, Urs Granacher (2015)
- *Sports medicine (Auckland, N.Z.)*
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0385-9
- Summary: Dose-response analysis specific to older adults. Found that moderate-to-high intensity RT (≥60% 1RM) with moderate volume (2-3 sets, 2-3x/week) produces optimal strength and functional outcomes in healthy older adults. Lower intensities can still produce benefits but with smaller effect sizes. Training periods of ≥12 weeks showed greater effects.

**5. Effects of Resistance Training Overload Progression Protocols on Strength and Muscle Mass.**
- Talisson Chaves, Maíra Scarpelli, João Bergamasco et al. (2024)
- *International journal of sports medicine*
- https://doi.org/10.1055/a-2256-5857
- Summary: Compared increasing load (LOADprog) versus increasing repetitions (REPSprog) as progressive overload strategies. Both protocols produced similar increases in muscle strength and cross-sectional area. Suggests that progressive overload can be achieved through multiple pathways — adding reps is as effective as adding weight for driving adaptation.

**6. Progressive overload without progressing load? The effects of load or repetition progression on muscular adaptations.**
- Daniel Plotkin, Max Coleman, Derrick Van Every et al. (2022)
- *PeerJ*
- https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.14142
- Summary: Examined whether progression in repetitions (rather than load) can drive continued muscular adaptations. Found that progressing repetitions to the top of a rep range (e.g., 8-12 reps) before increasing load produced similar hypertrophy and strength gains to traditional load-based progression. Supports flexible approaches to progressive overload.
