# Synthesis: Resistance Training for Health in Active 40+ Adults

## Evidence-Based Summary for Personal Trainers

### The Bottom Line

Resistance training is one of the most impactful interventions for health maintenance in adults over 40. The evidence consistently shows that relatively modest training volumes produce meaningful health benefits, and that the fundamental principles of programming are well-established. This synthesis distills findings from 5 topic areas into actionable guidance.

---

## 1. Minimum Effective Dose — Less Than You Think

**What the evidence says:**
- 1-2 sets per muscle group per week produces meaningful strength gains in untrained adults (Krieger, 2010; Androulakis-Korakakis et al., 2020)
- Even 1 RT session per week is associated with significant mortality risk reduction (Shailendra et al., 2022; Shailendra et al., 2024)
- The dose-response curve shows diminishing returns beyond ~10 sets per muscle group per week (Schoenfeld et al., 2017)
- Strength and health benefits have a lower minimum dose than hypertrophy

**Practical takeaway:** For health, 2 sessions per week with 2-3 sets per movement pattern is enough to get the majority of benefits. Clients who "don't have time" can still get meaningful results with minimal volume.

---

## 2. Programming Variables — What Actually Matters

**What the evidence says:**
- Volume is the primary driver of adaptation (Schoenfeld et al., 2019; Pelland et al., 2026)
- Frequency of 2x/week per muscle group is slightly superior to 1x/week when volume is equated, but the difference is small (Schoenfeld et al., 2019)
- Intensity of 60-80% 1RM is effective; lower intensities still produce benefits, especially in untrained individuals (Rhea et al., 2003; Borde et al., 2015)
- Progressive overload can be achieved by adding reps OR load — both are effective (Plotkin et al., 2022; Chaves et al., 2024)
- Tempo, rest periods, and rep ranges are less important for health than total volume and intensity

**Practical takeaway:** Focus on the big rocks: get enough sets done at reasonable intensity, and progress over time. Don't overthink tempo or exact rest periods for health-focused clients.

---

## 3. Concurrent Training — RT + Sport/Aerobic

**What the evidence says:**
- The interference effect is real but modest, primarily affecting maximal strength/hypertrophy (Wilson et al., 2012)
- Running causes more interference than cycling (Wilson et al., 2012)
- For untrained or moderately trained individuals, interference is minimal (Huiberts et al., 2024)
- Sprint/high-intensity endurance work causes less interference than long slow distance (Ferraro-Farro et al., 2026)
- Concurrent training produces similar fat loss to aerobic training alone while preserving lean mass (Lafontant et al., 2025)

**Practical takeaway:** Don't let clients skip RT because they're "already active." Adding RT to an aerobic/sport routine enhances health outcomes with minimal interference. Separate sessions by 6+ hours when possible, and prioritize RT before endurance if both are in one session.

---

## 4. Exercise Selection — Keep It Simple

**What the evidence says:**
- Multi-joint and single-joint exercises produce similar strength gains when volume is equated (Brigatto et al., 2020)
- Multi-joint exercises are more time-efficient (train multiple muscle groups at once)
- Free weights and machines produce comparable hypertrophy and strength outcomes (Haugen et al., 2023)
- Exercise selection matters less than load, volume, and proximity to failure (Currier et al., 2023)
- Suspension training and bodyweight exercises are viable alternatives (Soligon et al., 2020)
- Training specificity matters for functional outcomes (Stien et al., 2020)

**Practical takeaway:** Build programs around major movement patterns (squat, hinge, push, pull, carry). Use whatever equipment the client has access to and enjoys. Don't waste time on exercise variety for its own sake — a few well-chosen compounds done consistently beat a rotating list of exercises.

---

## 5. Recovery and Adaptation — Manage the Whole Picture

**What the evidence says:**
- Recovery capacity declines with age but 2-3 sessions/week is manageable for most 40+ adults (Mcleod et al., 2024; Radaelli et al., 2025)
- Sleep is a critical recovery variable — inadequate sleep impairs strength adaptations and hormonal responses (Knowles et al., 2018)
- Protein intake of ≥1.2-1.6 g/kg/day supports muscle mass and recovery (Nunes et al., 2022)
- Recovery from muscle damage is slower in older adults (Clifford, 2019)
- Consistency matters more than intensity — breaks in training reverse health benefits (Bing et al., 2025)

**Practical takeaway:** Program 2-3 sessions per week with 48-72 hours between sessions for the same movement patterns. Ask about sleep and stress — these are often the limiting factors, not the training itself. Prioritize protein intake and help clients build sustainable habits rather than aggressive programs they can't maintain.

---

## Programming Template for Health in 40+ Adults

Based on the evidence, an effective minimal program for health:

**Frequency:** 2-3 sessions per week
**Volume:** 2-3 sets per movement pattern (squat, hinge, push, pull, carry)
**Intensity:** 60-80% 1RM (or RPE 6-8 out of 10)
**Progression:** Add reps within a range, then increase load when top of range is achieved
**Duration:** 30-45 minutes per session
**Key movements:** Squat or lunge, hip hinge (deadlift/KB swing), horizontal/vertical push, horizontal/vertical pull, loaded carry

This represents the "minimum effective dose" that captures the majority of health benefits: reduced all-cause mortality, improved metabolic health, maintained bone density, preserved muscle mass and functional capacity, and reduced risk of falls and injury.
