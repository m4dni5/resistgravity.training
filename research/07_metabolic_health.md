## 07 — Metabolic Health (Diabetes, Insulin Sensitivity, Obesity)

**Key finding:** Resistance training improves insulin sensitivity, glycemic control (HbA1c), and body composition in both healthy individuals and those with type 2 diabetes. Skeletal muscle is the primary site of insulin-stimulated glucose uptake — more muscle = better metabolic health.

---

### Papers

**1. Unraveling Skeletal Muscle Insulin Resistance: Molecular Mechanisms and the Restorative Role of Exercise**
- Whytock KL, Goodpaster BH (2025)
- *Circulation Research* 136(4):e48-e68
- https://doi.org/10.1161/CIRCRESAHA.125.325532
- Summary: Comprehensive review of how skeletal muscle becomes insulin resistant and how exercise (especially resistance training) restores insulin sensitivity. Skeletal muscle accounts for ~80% of insulin-stimulated glucose uptake. Resistance training increases GLUT4 translocation, glycogen storage capacity, and mitochondrial function.

**2. Strength Training is More Effective Than Aerobic Exercise for Improving Glycaemic Control and Body Composition in Normal-Weight Type 2 Diabetes: RCT**
- Kobayashi Y, Long J, Dan S, et al. (2023)
- *Diabetologia* 66(9):1676-1686
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s00125-023-05958-9
- Summary: RCT showing that resistance training was superior to aerobic exercise for improving HbA1c and body composition in normal-weight individuals with type 2 diabetes. Challenges the assumption that aerobic exercise is always preferred for glycemic control.

**3. Exercise Training Modalities in Patients with Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus: Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis**
- Pan B, Ge L, Xun Y-Q, et al. (2018)
- *International Journal of Behavioral Nutrition and Physical Activity* 15(1):63
- https://doi.org/10.1186/s12966-018-0703-3
- Summary: Network meta-analysis comparing exercise types for T2DM. Combined aerobic + resistance training was most effective for HbA1c reduction, but resistance training alone showed significant improvements in glycemic control, comparable to aerobic training.

**4. Effects of Combined Aerobic and Resistance Training on Glycemic Control, Blood Pressure, Inflammation, Cardiorespiratory Fitness and Quality of Life in T2DM Patients with Overweight/Obesity: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis**
- Al-Mhanna SB, Batrakoulis A, Ghazali WNW, et al. (2024)
- *PeerJ* 12:e17525
- https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.17525
- Summary: Meta-analysis demonstrating that combined exercise improves glucose homeostasis, chronic systemic inflammation, cardiovascular function, and mental health in T2DM patients with obesity. Resistance training was a critical component of the combined protocols.

**5. Aerobic or Resistance Exercise, or Both, in Dieting Obese Older Adults**
- Villareal DT, Aguirre L, Gurney AB, et al. (2017)
- *New England Journal of Medicine* 376(20):1943-1955
- https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1616338
- Summary: Landmark NEJM RCT. In obese older adults on a weight-loss diet, resistance training prevented the loss of lean mass and bone density that occurred with diet alone or diet + aerobic exercise. The resistance training group maintained physical function while losing fat. Essential evidence for why older adults who lose weight must do resistance training.

**6. Resistance Training Effectiveness on Body Composition and Body Weight Outcomes in Individuals with Overweight/Obesity Across the Lifespan: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis**
- Lopez P, Taaffe DR, Galvão DA, et al. (2022)
- *Obesity Reviews* 23(7):e13428
- https://doi.org/10.1111/obr.13428
- Summary: Comprehensive meta-analysis across the lifespan. Resistance training consistently reduced body fat percentage and preserved or increased lean mass in overweight/obese individuals. Does not always produce significant weight loss on the scale, but dramatically improves body composition — a critical distinction for health outcomes.
