## 15 — Safety, Efficacy, and Population-Specific Responses

**Key finding:** Resistance training has one of the lowest injury rates of any physical activity (0.003-0.01 injuries per 1,000 training hours). It is safe and effective across all populations — youth, elderly, women, cardiac patients, those with osteoporosis, and people with neurological conditions. Both sexes and all races/ethnicities benefit.

---

### Papers

**1. Which Resistance Training is Safest to Practice? A Systematic Review**
- Serafim T, de Oliveira E, Maffulli N, et al. (2023)
- *Journal of Orthopaedic Surgery and Research* 18:478
- https://doi.org/10.1186/s13018-023-03781-x
- Summary: Systematic review of injury rates across resistance training modalities. Found that resistance training generally has very low injury rates compared to most sports and recreational activities. Machine-based training has the lowest injury rate, followed by free weights. Most injuries are minor (strains, sprains) and related to improper technique or excessive loading rather than inherent risk of RT itself.

**2. Injury Rate and Patterns in Group Strength-Endurance Training Classes**
- Batterson AR, Froelich RL, Schleck CD, et al. (2020)
- *Mayo Clinic Proceedings* 95(7):1432-1439
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mayocp.2019.03.032
- Summary: Prospective study of 100 adults in group strength-endurance training. Injury rate was 0.27 injuries per 1,000 training hours — very low compared to recreational sports. Most injuries were minor muscle strains. The injury rate in resistance training is a fraction of that seen in running, basketball, soccer, or even recreational walking.

**3. Sex Differences in Resistance Training: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis**
- Roberts BM, Nuckols G, Krieger JW (2020)
- *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* 34(5):1448-1460
- https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0000000000003521
- Summary: Meta-analysis comparing male and female responses to resistance training. Both sexes experience significant improvements in strength and hypertrophy. Relative hypertrophy gains are similar between sexes, but absolute gains are greater in men due to higher baseline muscle mass and hormonal environment. Women can train with the same programs as men and achieve comparable relative adaptations. This is powerful evidence countering the myth that women should "train differently" or "lift lighter."

**4. Resistance Training Responses Across Race and Ethnicity: A Narrative Review**
- Tarar A, Knox A, Dean C, et al. (2023)
- *Ethnicity & Health* 28(7):1009-1027
- https://doi.org/10.1080/13557858.2023.2212147
- Summary: Review examining racial/ethnic differences in RT outcomes. All racial and ethnic groups benefit from resistance training for cardiometabolic health, body composition, and strength. Some differences exist in the magnitude of specific outcomes (e.g., body composition changes), but the direction of benefit is universal. Resistance training is equally recommended for all populations.

**5. Youth Resistance Training: Safety, Efficacy, and Injury Prevention Effects**
- Faigenbaum AD, Myer GD (2010)
- *British Journal of Sports Medicine* 44(1):56-63
- https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsm.2009.068098
- Summary: Comprehensive review establishing that youth resistance training is safe and effective when properly supervised. Injury rates are very low (lower than many youth sports). Resistance training does NOT stunt growth, damage growth plates, or cause excessive hypertrophy in youth. Instead, it improves strength, body composition, bone density, motor skills, sports performance, and psychological well-being. The myth that children should not lift weights is not supported by evidence.

**6. Weight Training in Youth: Growth, Maturation, and Safety — An Evidence-Based Review**
- Malina RM (2006)
- *Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine* 16(6):504-506
- https://doi.org/10.1097/01.jsm.0000248843.31874.be
- Summary: Evidence-based review confirming that resistance training in pre- and early-pubertal youth does not adversely affect growth, maturation, or the musculoskeletal system. Supervised training with proper technique is associated with fewer injuries than many popular youth sports.

**7. Effects of Resistance Training on Muscle Size and Strength in Very Elderly Adults (≥75 years): Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of RCTs**
- Grgic J, Garofolini A, Orazem J, et al. (2020)
- *Sports Medicine* 50(7):1337-1347
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-020-01331-7
- Summary: Meta-analysis specifically in the very elderly (75+ years). Resistance training produced significant improvements in muscle size and strength even in this age group. Adverse events were rare and minor. Demonstrates that it is never too late to start resistance training.

**8. Heavy Resistance Training is Safe and Improves Bone, Function, and Stature in Postmenopausal Women with Low to Very Low Bone Mass: The LIFTMOR Trial**
- Watson SL, Weeks BK, Weis LJ, et al. (2015)
- *Osteoporosis International* 26(12):2729-2737
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s00198-015-3263-2
- Summary: The landmark LIFTMOR trial demonstrated that high-intensity resistance and impact training is SAFE even in postmenopausal women with osteoporosis — the population many clinicians worry about most. No injuries or adverse events occurred during supervised high-intensity training. Furthermore, it improved bone mineral density, physical function, and even stature (reducing kyphosis). Directly challenges the outdated advice to "be careful" or "don't lift heavy" with osteoporosis.

**9. High-Intensity Resistance and Impact Training Improves Bone Mineral Density and Physical Function in Postmenopausal Women With Osteopenia and Osteoporosis: The LIFTMOR RCT**
- Watson SL, Weeks BK, Weis LJ, et al. (2018)
- *Journal of Bone and Mineral Research* 33(2):211-220
- https://doi.org/10.1002/jbmr.3284
- Summary: Full RCT results from LIFTMOR. 8-month supervised high-intensity RT + impact loading improved femoral neck and lumbar spine BMD, functional performance, and muscle strength in postmenopausal women with low bone mass. Critically, no adverse events occurred — demonstrating that supervised high-intensity RT is safe for osteoporotic populations.

**10. Strong, Steady and Straight: UK Consensus Statement on Physical Activity and Exercise for Osteoporosis**
- Brooke-Wavell K, Skelton DA, Barker KL, et al. (2022)
- *British Journal of Sports Medicine* 56(16):913-919
- https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2021-104634
- Summary: UK consensus statement explicitly addressing safety concerns. States that resistance training and impact exercise are safe for people with osteoporosis when appropriately prescribed. The consensus directly addresses the fear that exercise will cause fractures, concluding that the fracture risk from inactivity far exceeds any risk from properly prescribed exercise.

**11. Cluster Sets to Prescribe Interval Resistance Training: A Potential Method to Optimise Resistance Training Safety, Feasibility and Efficacy in Cardiac Patients**
- Way KL, Thomas HJ, Parker L, et al. (2023)
- *Sports Medicine - Open* 9:79
- https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-023-00634-z
- Summary: Reviews evidence that resistance training is safe and beneficial for cardiac patients. Cluster set protocols (inter-set rest periods) can optimize safety while maintaining efficacy. Resistance training improves muscle mass, maintains functional capacity, and enhances quality of life in cardiac rehabilitation patients.

**12. Physical Exercise for People with Parkinson's Disease: Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis (Cochrane)**
- Ernst M, Folkerts A-K, Gollan R, et al. (2023/2024)
- *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews* CD013856
- https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD013856.pub3
- Summary: Cochrane systematic review confirming that exercise (including resistance training) is safe and effective for people with Parkinson's disease. Resistance training improves motor function, muscle strength, balance, and quality of life. No serious adverse events reported. Supports RT as a complementary therapy for neurodegenerative conditions.

**13. Canadian Guideline for Physical Activity Throughout Pregnancy**
- Mottola MI, Davenport MH, Ruchat S-M, et al. (2018/2019)
- *Journal of Obstetrics and Gynaecology Canada* 40(11):1528-1537
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jogc.2018.07.001
- Summary: Official Canadian guidelines explicitly recommending resistance/strength training during pregnancy. Pregnant women should perform moderate-intensity resistance training at least 3 times per week. Evidence shows RT during pregnancy is safe, reduces gestational diabetes risk, reduces excessive gestational weight gain, and does not increase adverse birth outcomes.

**14. Exercise for Preventing Falls in Older People Living in the Community (Cochrane Review)**
- Sherrington C, Fairhall NJ, Wallbank GK, et al. (2019)
- *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews* CD012424
- https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD012424.pub2
- Summary: Definitive Cochrane review (108 RCTs, 23,407 participants). Exercise programs — particularly those including resistance/strength and balance training — reduce the rate of falls by 23%. Programs of ≥3 hours per week of exercise are most effective. Confirms that the exercise intervention itself (including resistance training) is safe, with falls being prevented rather than caused.
