## 02 — Mortality & Longevity

**Key finding:** Muscular strength is an independent predictor of all-cause mortality, even after controlling for age, BMI, smoking, and cardiovascular risk factors. The association is dose-dependent.

---

### Papers

**1. Muscular Strength as a Predictor of All-Cause Mortality in an Apparently Healthy Population: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Data From ~2 Million Men and Women**
- García-Hermoso A, Cavero-Redondo I, Ramírez-Vélez R, et al. (2018)
- *Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation* 99(11):2100-2113
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmr.2018.01.008
- Summary: Meta-analysis of prospective studies with approximately 2 million participants. Higher muscular strength was associated with a 10-17% lower risk of all-cause mortality. The protective effect was observed in both men and women. Grip strength and knee extension strength were both significant predictors.

**2. Association Between Muscular Strength and Mortality in Men: Prospective Cohort Study**
- Ruiz JR, Sui X, Lobelo F, et al. (2008)
- *BMJ* 337:a439
- https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.a439
- Summary: Landmark prospective cohort of 8,762 men aged 20-80 followed for 18.9 years. All-cause mortality risk was 60-78% lower in the highest vs lowest muscular strength tertile, even after adjusting for age, physical activity, smoking, alcohol, BMI, and comorbidities. The association held for cardiovascular mortality and cancer mortality.

**3. Thresholds of Handgrip Strength for All-Cause, Cancer, and Cardiovascular Mortality: Systematic Review with Dose-Response Meta-Analysis**
- López-Bueno R, Andersen LL, Koyanagi A, et al. (2022)
- *Ageing Research Reviews* 81:101778
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2022.101778
- Summary: Dose-response meta-analysis establishing that handgrip strength shows a clear threshold effect — mortality risk increases sharply below certain grip strength values, with the curve flattening at higher values. Provides practical benchmarks for clinicians.

**4. Association of Muscle Wasting with Mortality Risk Among Adults: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Prospective Studies**
- Zhou H-H, Liao Y, Peng Z, et al. (2023)
- *Journal of Cachexia, Sarcopenia and Muscle* 14(4):1617-1628
- https://doi.org/10.1002/jcsm.13263
- Summary: Meta-analysis of prospective studies quantifying that muscle wasting is associated with significantly increased all-cause mortality risk (HR 1.6-2.0). Confirms that preserving muscle mass is a survival factor.

**5. Association Between Muscular Strength and Mortality in Clinical Populations: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis**
- Jochem C, Leitzmann M, Volaklis K, et al. (2019)
- *Journal of the American Medical Directors Association* 20(10):1210-1218
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jamda.2019.05.015
- Summary: Extends the strength-mortality association to clinical populations (cancer, COPD, renal disease, CVD). Confirms that muscular strength predicts survival even in diseased populations.

**6. Dose-Response Association Between Handgrip Strength and All-Cause Mortality Across Different Levels of Systemic Inflammation**
- Tur-Boned A, Andersen LL, López-Bueno R, et al. (2026)
- *Journal of Cachexia, Sarcopenia and Muscle*
- https://doi.org/10.1002/jcsm.70272
- Summary: Recent study showing that handgrip strength maintains its dose-response protective association with mortality even at high levels of systemic inflammation (CRP). Suggests the benefits of muscle strength are independent of inflammatory status.
