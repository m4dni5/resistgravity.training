## 04 — Cognitive & Brain Health

**Key finding:** Resistance training improves executive function, memory, and processing speed in older adults. The benefits appear independent of and additive to aerobic exercise. Leg strength is a particularly strong predictor of cognitive function.

---

### Papers

**1. Resistance Training and Executive Functions: A 12-Month Randomized Controlled Trial**
- Liu-Ambrose T, Nagamatsu LS, Graf P, et al. (2010)
- *Archives of Internal Medicine* 170(2):170-178
- https://doi.org/10.1001/archinternmed.2009.494
- Summary: Landmark RCT of 155 women aged 65-75. Both once-weekly and twice-weekly resistance training improved executive functions (Stroop test, Trail Making) compared to balance-and-tone controls. Effects persisted 12 months after training ended. Demonstrates that resistance training selectively improves cognitive processes mediated by frontal lobe regions.

**2. Exercise Interventions for Cognitive Function in Adults Older Than 50: Systematic Review with Meta-Analysis**
- Northey JM, Cherbuin N, Pumpa KL, et al. (2018)
- *British Journal of Sports Medicine* 52(3):154-160
- https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2016-096587
- Summary: Meta-analysis of 39 RCTs (n=3,359). Exercise significantly improved cognitive function in adults over 50, regardless of cognitive status. Resistance training and aerobic exercise were both effective. Combined exercise programs showed the strongest effects.

**3. Effects of Resistance Exercise Training on Cognitive Function and Physical Performance in Cognitive Frailty: A Randomized Controlled Trial**
- Yoon DH, Lee J-Y, Song W (2018)
- *The Journal of Nutrition, Health & Aging* 22(10):1248-1254
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s12603-018-1090-9
- Summary: RCT showing 6 months of resistance training improved cognitive function (MMSE scores) and physical performance in older adults with cognitive frailty. Demonstrates that RT can reverse early-stage cognitive decline.

**4. Optimal Dose and Type of Exercise to Improve Cognitive Function in Older Adults: Systematic Review and Bayesian Network Meta-Analysis**
- Gallardo-Gómez D, Del Pozo-Cruz J, Noetel M, et al. (2022)
- *Ageing Research Reviews* 80:101591
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.arr.2022.101591
- Summary: Network meta-analysis examining dose-response. Resistance training at moderate-to-vigorous intensity for 45-60 minutes, 2-3 times per week, was optimal for cognitive improvement in older adults. Combined resistance + aerobic was most effective overall.

**5. Possible Neuroprotective Mechanisms of Physical Exercise in Neurodegeneration**
- Mahalakshmi B, Maurya N, Lee S-D, et al. (2020)
- *International Journal of Molecular Sciences* 21(16):5895
- https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms21165895
- Summary: Review of molecular mechanisms. Exercise (including resistance training) promotes neuroprotection through BDNF upregulation, increased cerebral blood flow, reduced neuroinflammation, enhanced neuroplasticity, and improved mitochondrial function in brain tissue.

**6. The Impact of Resistance Training on Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor and Depression Among Older Adults: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of RCTs**
- Setayesh S, Mohammad Rahimi GR (2023)
- *Geriatric Nursing* 53:225-232
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gerinurse.2023.08.022
- Summary: Meta-analysis showing resistance training significantly increases serum BDNF levels in older adults. BDNF is a key neurotrophin supporting neurogenesis, synaptic plasticity, and cognitive function. Provides a mechanistic link between RT and brain health.
