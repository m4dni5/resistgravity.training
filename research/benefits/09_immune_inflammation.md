## 09 — Immune Function & Inflammation

**Key finding:** Skeletal muscle is an endocrine organ that secretes myokines — signaling molecules with anti-inflammatory effects. Resistance training reduces chronic low-grade inflammation (IL-6, CRP, TNF-α), which underlies many age-related diseases.

---

### Papers

**1. Muscles, Exercise and Obesity: Skeletal Muscle as a Secretory Organ**
- Pedersen BK, Febbraio MA (2012)
- *Nature Reviews Endocrinology* 8(8):457-465
- https://doi.org/10.1038/nrendo.2012.49
- Summary: Foundational review establishing that contracting skeletal muscle releases myokines (IL-6, IL-8, IL-15, irisin, BDNF, and others) that act in paracrine and endocrine fashion. Muscle-derived IL-6 has anti-inflammatory effects, stimulating IL-1ra and IL-10 while inhibiting TNF-α. Reframes muscle as a protective organ, not just a locomotor. (Highly cited)

**2. The Anti-Inflammatory Effect of Exercise**
- Petersen AMW, Pedersen BK (2005)
- *Journal of Applied Physiology* 98(4):1154-1162
- https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00164.2004
- Summary: Landmark paper explaining that regular exercise reduces chronic systemic inflammation — a root cause of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, cancer, and dementia. The anti-inflammatory effect is mediated through muscle-derived IL-6 and other myokines. Resistance exercise is specifically shown to produce this response.

**3. The Role of IL-6 in Mediating the Anti-Inflammatory Effects of Exercise**
- Petersen AMW, Pedersen BK (2007)
- *Journal of Physiology and Pharmacology* 58(Suppl 5):41-52
- PubMed: 17242490
- Summary: Detailed mechanistic paper showing that muscle-derived IL-6 released during exercise has anti-inflammatory properties: it inhibits TNF-α production, stimulates IL-1ra and IL-10, and enhances insulin sensitivity. Resistance exercise produces significant IL-6 release from working muscles.

**4. Immune Function in Sport and Exercise**
- Gleeson M (2007)
- *Journal of Applied Physiology* 103(2):693-699
- https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00008.2007
- Summary: Review showing that regular moderate exercise (including resistance training) reduces incidence of infection. While prolonged strenuous exercise can transiently suppress immunity, habitual moderate exercise enhances immune surveillance. Resistance training at moderate intensity supports immune function.

**5. Dose-Response Association Between Handgrip Strength and All-Cause Mortality Across Different Levels of Systemic Inflammation**
- Tur-Boned A, Andersen LL, López-Bueno R, et al. (2026)
- *Journal of Cachexia, Sarcopenia and Muscle*
- https://doi.org/10.1002/jcsm.70272
- Summary: Shows that higher muscle strength provides mortality protection even in individuals with high systemic inflammation (high CRP). Suggests that muscle mass/strength has protective effects independent of inflammation status — a powerful argument for strength training in chronically inflamed populations.

**6. Effects of Exercise on Inflammation in Female Survivors of Nonmetastatic Breast Cancer**
- Bettariga F, Taaffe DR, Borsati A, et al. (2025)
- *Journal of the National Cancer Institute*
- https://doi.org/10.1093/jnci/djaf062
- Summary: Recent meta-analysis showing exercise reduces CRP and IL-6 in breast cancer survivors. Extends the anti-inflammatory evidence to clinical populations where chronic inflammation drives disease progression.
