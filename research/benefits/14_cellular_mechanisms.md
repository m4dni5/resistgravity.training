## 14 — Cellular & Molecular Mechanisms

**Key finding:** Resistance training triggers a cascade of molecular events — muscle protein synthesis, myokine release, hormonal responses, mitochondrial biogenesis, and autophagy — that explain its wide-ranging health benefits. Understanding mechanisms strengthens the evidence for prescribing RT.

---

### Papers

**1. Stimuli and Sensors That Initiate Skeletal Muscle Hypertrophy Following Resistance Exercise**
- Wackerhage H, Schoenfeld BJ, Hamilton DL, et al. (2019)
- *Journal of Applied Physiology* 126(3):517-525
- https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.00685.2018
- Summary: Comprehensive review of the molecular cascade from mechanical loading to muscle growth. Resistance exercise activates mechanosensors (titin, integrins) → mTORC1 signaling → increased ribosomal biogenesis and muscle protein synthesis → hypertrophy. Also discusses the roles of hormones (testosterone, growth hormone, IGF-1) and satellite cells.

**2. A Review of Resistance Training-Induced Changes in Skeletal Muscle Protein Synthesis and Their Contribution to Hypertrophy**
- Damas F, Phillips SM, Vechin FC, et al. (2015)
- *Sports Medicine* 45(6):767-783
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-015-0320-0
- Summary: Review of how resistance exercise stimulates muscle protein synthesis (MPS). RE + protein ingestion produces additive MPS stimulation. The cumulative effect of repeated bouts of elevated MPS drives hypertrophy over time. Important for understanding why nutrition timing matters.

**3. Resistance Training-Induced Changes in Integrated Myofibrillar Protein Synthesis Are Related to Hypertrophy After Attenuation of Muscle Damage**
- Damas F, Phillips SM, Libardi CA, et al. (2016)
- *The Journal of Physiology* 594(18):5209-5222
- https://doi.org/10.1113/JP272472
- Summary: Shows that early resistance training adaptations are driven primarily by neural and connective tissue changes, with muscle hypertrophy becoming significant only after initial muscle damage attenuates. Explains why hypertrophy "lags" behind strength gains in early training.

**4. Hormonal Responses and Adaptations to Resistance Exercise and Training**
- Kraemer WJ, Ratamess NA (2005)
- *Sports Medicine* 35(4):339-361
- https://doi.org/10.2165/00007256-200535040-00004
- Summary: Comprehensive review of acute hormonal responses to resistance exercise: testosterone, growth hormone, cortisol, IGF-1. Heavy resistance exercise produces transient hormonal elevations that support tissue remodeling and growth. The acute hormonal response appears more important than chronic resting hormone levels.

**5. Muscles, Exercise and Obesity: Skeletal Muscle as a Secretory Organ**
- Pedersen BK, Febbraio MA (2012)
- *Nature Reviews Endocrinology* 8(8):457-465
- https://doi.org/10.1038/nrendo.2012.49
- Summary: Foundational paper establishing that contracting muscle releases >300 myokines with paracrine and endocrine effects. These include anti-inflammatory (IL-6, IL-10), metabolic (irisin), neurotrophic (BDNF), and anti-cancer (IL-15) factors. Reframes muscle as a protective endocrine organ. (Highly cited)

**6. Human Muscle Protein Synthesis and Breakdown During and After Exercise**
- Kumar V, Atherton P, Smith K, et al. (2009)
- *Journal of Applied Physiology* 106(6):2026-2039
- https://doi.org/10.1152/japplphysiol.91481.2008
- Summary: Detailed examination of muscle protein turnover during and after exercise of different modes and intensities. Resistance exercise produces the largest and most sustained increases in mixed and myofibrillar protein synthesis. Protein intake amplifies the response. Establishes the fundamental physiological basis for resistance training adaptations.
