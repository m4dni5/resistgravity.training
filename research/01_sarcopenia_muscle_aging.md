## 01 — Sarcopenia & Muscle Aging

**Key finding:** Adults lose 3-8% of muscle mass per decade after age 30, accelerating after 60. Resistance training is the most effective intervention to reverse or slow this decline.

---

### Papers

**1. Resistance Training is Medicine: Effects of Strength Training on Health**
- Westcott WL (2012)
- *Current Sports Medicine Reports*
- https://doi.org/10.1249/JSR.0b013e31825dabb8
- Summary: Inactive adults lose 3-8% of muscle mass per decade, with resting metabolic rate reduction and fat accumulation. Ten weeks of resistance training can increase lean weight by 1.4 kg, increase resting metabolic rate by 7%, and reduce fat by 1.8 kg. Resistance training also improves cardiovascular health, bone density, glucose metabolism, and mental health. Argues resistance training should be prescribed like medicine.

**2. Benefits of Resistance Training in Early and Late Stages of Frailty and Sarcopenia: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis**
- Talar K, Hernández-Belmonte A, Větrovský T, et al. (2021)
- *Journal of Clinical Medicine* 10(8):1630
- https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm10081630
- Summary: Systematic review of RCTs showing resistance training improves muscle strength, physical performance, and body composition in both early and late stages of frailty and sarcopenia. Benefits are present even in the most frail older adults. (145 citations)

**3. Exercise for Sarcopenia in Older People: A Systematic Review and Network Meta-Analysis**
- Shen Y, Shi Q, Nong K, et al. (2023)
- *Journal of Cachexia, Sarcopenia and Muscle* 14(3):1193-1205
- https://doi.org/10.1002/jcsm.13255
- Summary: Network meta-analysis comparing exercise types for sarcopenia. Resistance training outperforms aerobic exercise for improving muscle mass and strength in older adults with sarcopenia. Combined resistance + aerobic shows additional benefits for physical function.

**4. Effects of Resistance Training in Healthy Older People with Sarcopenia: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of RCTs**
- Chen N, He X, Feng Y, et al. (2021)
- *European Review of Aging and Physical Activity* 18:16
- https://doi.org/10.1186/s11556-021-00277-7
- Summary: Meta-analysis of 19 RCTs. Resistance training significantly improved appendicular lean mass, handgrip strength, gait speed, and chair stand time in older adults with sarcopenia. Supports RT as primary intervention.

**5. Sarcopenia Definition, Diagnosis and Treatment: Consensus is Growing**
- Sayer AA, Cruz-Jentoft A (2022)
- *Age and Ageing* 51(7):afac220
- https://doi.org/10.1093/ageing/afac220
- Summary: Consensus paper defining sarcopenia as a skeletal muscle disorder involving loss of muscle strength, mass, and function. Reviews diagnostic criteria (EWGSOP2) and establishes resistance exercise + protein intake as first-line treatment.

**6. Resistance Training Volume on Physical Function, Lean Body Mass and Lower-Body Muscle Hypertrophy and Strength in Older Adults: Network Meta-analysis of 151 RCTs**
- Radaelli R, Rech A, Molinari T, et al. (2025)
- *Sports Medicine* 55:151-168
- https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-024-02123-z
- Summary: Massive network meta-analysis of 151 randomized trials. Found resistance training volume of 2-6 sets per muscle group per week, performed 2-3 times weekly, optimizes lean body mass and functional outcomes in older adults.

**7. Global Consensus on Optimal Exercise Recommendations for Enhancing Healthy Longevity in Older Adults (ICFSR)**
- Izquierdo M, de Souto Barreto P, Arai H, et al. (2025)
- *The Journal of Nutrition, Health & Aging*
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jnha.2024.100401
- Summary: International consensus from the International Conference on Frailty and Sarcopenia Research. Recommends progressive resistance training as a core component of exercise programs for older adults, alongside balance and flexibility work. Evidence level: strong.
