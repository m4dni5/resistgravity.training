## 06 — Cardiovascular Health

**Key finding:** Resistance training reduces resting blood pressure by 3-6 mmHg systolic, improves lipid profiles, reduces arterial stiffness, and lowers cardiovascular disease risk independently of aerobic exercise.

---

### Papers

**1. Exercise Training and Resting Blood Pressure: Large-Scale Pairwise and Network Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials**
- Edwards JJ, Deenmamode AHP, Griffiths M, et al. (2023)
- *British Journal of Sports Medicine* 57(20):1317-1326
- https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2022-106503
- Summary: Massive meta-analysis (n=15,827) across all exercise modalities. Both dynamic resistance training and isometric resistance training significantly reduced resting systolic blood pressure. Isometric wall squats and isometric handgrip were surprisingly effective. Combined training was optimal, but resistance training alone produced clinically meaningful reductions.

**2. Chronic Effect of Resistance Training on Blood Pressure in Older Adults with Prehypertension and Hypertension: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis**
- Henkin J, Pinto R, Machado C, et al. (2023)
- *Experimental Gerontology* 180:112193
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.exger.2023.112193
- Summary: Meta-analysis of older adults specifically (≥60 years). Resistance training produced clinically significant reductions in both systolic and diastolic blood pressure in those with prehypertension and hypertension. Supports RT as an accessible non-pharmacological intervention for blood pressure management in the elderly.

**3. Exercise Training for Blood Pressure: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis**
- Cornelissen VA, Smart NA (2013)
- *Journal of the American Heart Association* 2(1):e004473
- https://doi.org/10.1161/JAHA.112.004473
- Summary: Comprehensive meta-analysis of all exercise types. Dynamic resistance training reduced systolic BP by 3.2 mmHg and diastolic by 2.0 mmHg. Isometric resistance training reduced systolic BP by 6.8 mmHg — a clinically remarkable finding. (Highly cited landmark paper)

**4. Aerobic, Resistance or Combined Training: Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Exercise to Reduce Cardiovascular Risk in Adults with Metabolic Syndrome**
- Wewege MA, Thom JM, Rye K-A, et al. (2018)
- *Atherosclerosis* 274:162-171
- https://doi.org/10.1016/j.atherosclerosis.2018.05.002
- Summary: Meta-analysis in metabolic syndrome populations. All exercise modalities improved cardiovascular risk factors, with resistance training being particularly effective for improving blood pressure and lipid profiles. Supports RT as a viable option for those who cannot perform aerobic exercise.

**5. Sarcopenia and Cardiovascular Diseases**
- Damluji AA, Alfaraidhy M, AlHajri N, et al. (2023)
- *Circulation* 147(5):417-432
- https://doi.org/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.123.064071
- Summary: Comprehensive review from Circulation establishing the bidirectional relationship between sarcopenia and cardiovascular disease. Low muscle mass and strength independently predict CVD events. Resistance training is recommended as both prevention and rehabilitation for cardiovascular patients.

**6. Resistance Training and Cardiovascular Health: Epigenetic Regulation**
- Silva J, Rodrigues LF, Torres T, et al. (2026)
- *Frontiers in Physiology* 16:1701689
- https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2025.1701689
- Summary: Recent review examining epigenetic mechanisms (DNA methylation, histone modification, miRNA) through which resistance training improves cardiovascular health. RT modulates gene expression related to vascular function, inflammation, and cardiac remodeling.
