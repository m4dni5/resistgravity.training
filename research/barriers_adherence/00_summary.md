# 00 — Synthesis: Evidence-Based Implications for Service Design

## Overview

This synthesis draws from six research files covering barriers to RT adoption, adherence and dropout, time efficiency, beginner on-ramps, progression failures, and the psychology of exercise behavior. The evidence maps directly onto two target audiences for ResistGravity.Training.

---

## Audience 1: Non-Lifters Needing an On-Ramp

### What the evidence says

**Barriers are modifiable, not intrinsic.** The most commonly cited barriers — time, intimidation, lack of knowledge, fear of injury — are all addressable through program design (01_barriers_adoption.md). Adults 40+ don't avoid RT because they lack motivation; they face real informational and environmental obstacles.

**Time is the #1 barrier, but it's partly perceptual.** Low-volume RT (1-2 sets, 2-3x/week, ~30 min sessions) produces clinically meaningful strength and health gains (03_time_efficiency.md). The evidence supports a "minimal effective dose" approach that directly attacks the time objection.

**The first 8 weeks are critical.** Beginners show measurable strength gains within 4-8 weeks with proper guidance (04_onramp_beginners.md). Supervised instruction — even remote — significantly improves outcomes and reduces injury risk compared to unsupervised training.

**Simple beats complex for adoption.** Program complexity is a documented dropout predictor (02_adherence_dropout.md). Beginners should start with fewer exercises, simpler movement patterns, and clear progression rules.

**Identity precedes consistency.** Self-Determination Theory research shows that autonomy, competence, and relatedness are the strongest predictors of sustained exercise behavior (06_identity_psychology.md). A service that builds exercise identity ("I am someone who lifts") outperforms one that relies on external accountability alone.

### Service design implications

| Evidence | Design Response |
|----------|----------------|
| Time is the #1 barrier | Default program: 3x/week, ~30 min sessions |
| Intimidation/knowledge gaps | Structured on-ramp with form guidance, simple exercises |
| First 8 weeks are critical | Onboarding program specifically for weeks 1-8 |
| Simple > complex for beginners | 4-5 exercises max, linear progression, no periodization initially |
| Supervision improves outcomes | At minimum: remote check-ins, video form review |
| Identity builds adherence | Frame the service around becoming "a lifter," not just "exercising" |

## Audience 2: Existing Lifters Not Getting Results

### What the evidence says

**Progressive overload is the fundamental driver.** Many lifters train hard but without a structured progression plan (05_progression_failures.md). Without systematic load increases, the body adapts to the stimulus and progress stalls.

**Junk volume is pervasive.** Lifters often accumulate training volume that generates fatigue without driving adaptation — too many exercises, too many sets at insufficient intensity, or redundant movement patterns.

**Program hopping prevents adaptation.** Constantly changing exercises or programs disrupts the stimulus needed for progressive overload. Consistency in exercise selection with progressive loading is more effective than variety for strength gains.

**Concurrent activities create interference.** High-volume endurance training, recreational sports, or other physical activities can interfere with RT recovery and adaptation — a common issue for active adults who add RT to an already full schedule.

**Recovery is underappreciated.** Inadequate sleep, nutrition, and stress management are common reasons lifters stall. The problem is often outside the gym, not inside it.

**Dropout is driven by perceived lack of progress.** When lifters don't see results, motivation collapses. This creates a negative feedback loop: no results → less motivation → inconsistent training → fewer results.

### Service design implications

| Evidence | Design Response |
|----------|----------------|
| No progressive overload = no results | Build progression tracking into the service |
| Junk volume is common | Audit program for effective volume vs. fatigue |
| Program hopping disrupts adaptation | Emphasize consistency; change only when plateau is confirmed |
| Concurrent training interference | Assess total activity load, not just RT program |
| Recovery is the bottleneck | Include recovery assessment (sleep, nutrition, stress) |
| Perceived lack of progress drives dropout | Provide objective progress metrics and regular check-ins |

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. Simplicity is a feature, not a compromise
Across all six research areas, simpler programs consistently outperform complex ones for adherence, adoption, and even results (for beginners and intermediates). Complexity should be earned through progression, not imposed at the start.

### 2. The intention-action gap is the core problem
People know they should lift. The gap between intention and action is filled by modifiable barriers: knowledge, time perception, intimidation, and lack of structure. A service that systematically removes these barriers converts intention into behavior.

### 3. Autonomy-supportive > compliance-driven
Self-Determination Theory evidence is clear: programs that support choice, build competence, and create belonging produce better long-term outcomes than rigid, prescriptive approaches. The service should guide, not dictate.

### 4. Measurement enables motivation
Objective progress tracking (strength gains, volume progression, consistency metrics) addresses the perception-of-no-progress problem that drives dropout. What gets measured gets continued.

### 5. The 8-week window
Whether it's a new lifter starting or a stalled lifter restarting, the first 8 weeks are when the most critical gains — physical and psychological — occur. The service should be designed to maximize success in this window.

---

## Strategic Recommendations

1. **Build a clear on-ramp program** for non-lifters: simple, supervised (even remotely), 8 weeks to independence
2. **Default to minimal effective dose**: 3 sessions/week, 30-45 min, 4-5 exercises
3. **Include a diagnostic service** for stalled lifters: audit programming, recovery, progression, and total load
4. **Track and display progress**: strength metrics, consistency, volume over time
5. **Frame around identity**: "become a lifter" not "start exercising"
6. **Support autonomy**: provide principles and options, not rigid prescriptions
7. **Address recovery explicitly**: sleep, nutrition, and stress as part of the program
